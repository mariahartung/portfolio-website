Expert

Overview

Expert connects students to industry experts for career advice. The project was part of my certified UX design program by CareerFoundry. The goal was to create a responsive web app with a mobile-first approach, for anyone to instantly chat with experts in any field, to get advice on how to face their (everyday) problems. The web app is free to use but requires payment for any video calls done over the platform. Although the brief should be used as a guide, we had considerable freedom in finding a niche people could need expert advice in.

Project Background

I was motivated to create an app focused on career advice after I looked at the representative study "Schule-und dann?" by Institut für Demoskopie Allensbach from 2014. According to this study:

  • More than 50% of high school students have difficulties choosing a career path.
  • 20% of students don't have a clear idea of what career path to take after graduation.
  • 35% of them don't feel enough informed about job roles, apprenticeships, and study programs.

These insights inspired me to explore the problem further and create Expert for people who are unsure about their career choice yet.

The Challenge

Problem

Choosing a career path can be daunting because it's probably one of the most important decisions students can make in their lives. Finding relevant information on professions online can be time-consuming. Vague information on sites also make it hard to know what to expect from a role or if it's worth pursuing it. Asking family and friends for advice doesn't always help either.

Problem Statement

Students need a way to get answers to their career questions from experienced industry experts because they can't find relevant information online to get a realistic overview of job roles in order to make a career choice.

We will know this to be true when we see how many users use our app to get career advice from industry experts and recommend the app to others.

Solution

Potential Solution

Expert aims to solve this problem by connecting students to industry experts, so they can clarify their questions (via messages and calls) and get instant career advice to make an informed decision about what career path to take.

Find experienced career and industry experts in tech.

Instead of spending a lot of time browsing websites, the web app gives users instant access to experienced career and industry experts in tech they can reach out to for individual career advice. Users can easily sign up via email and search for experts related to professions they're interested in. Career experts provide guidance for users who don't have a clear idea about their career paths yet. Industry experts can answer specific questions on profession users seek advice in.

Send free messages and get instant career advice.

Users can get in touch with verified experts and send them free messages with their career questions to get relevant information they can't find online, such as on work tasks, entry requirements, salary, and more. Users can also check the experts' about me, professional experience, and reviews of others to estimate if the expert is trustworthy before they contact them for advice.

Book paid calls for inside information to get a realistic overview of job roles.

Users can book video call sessions with experts for in-depth advice to get inside information on roles they have in mind to get a realistic overview of professions and make the right career choice. To do so, users can upgrade to affordable monthly plans with different career coaching options and a certain quota of minutes for video calls.

Process

Design Thinking

During the project, I applied the design thinking methodology to understand the user, define the problem, and create the user-centered solution in six phases.

Understand

Analyzing the Competition

I started off by conducting a competitive analysis to understand the problem space and find ways Expert could have a competitive advantage in the market. For the analysis, I chose edggi as a direct and coachilla as an indirect competitor. The insights were then compiled into two SWOT profiles.

Opportunities for Expert

  • Clear labeling of navigation items.
  • Provide auto-complete and autosuggestions for search.
  • Original design instead of a copy from others.
  • Regular marketing for more visibility and app awareness.
  • Provide a forum feature to ask experts and view similar questions.

Observe

Survey

Next, I carried out user research to understand the user. I first ran an online survey with 16 participants, that was distributed on Facebook, Reddit, Slack, and thestudentroom.co.uk to reach a wider audience.

Main Goals

  • Identify the most important target audience of Expert.
  • Find out about pain points and tools used in searching for career advice online.
  • Prioritize features that would be of value to the target audience.

Key Results

  • Our primary users are between 25-34 years old and are either professionals or students.
  • The majority of users feel somewhat informed about their career choices and mainly searches online to find out about career options in the field they currently study for. Therefore, we can narrow down the target audience to college students.
  • Common pain points are no option to filter content and irrelevant content.
  • The most helpful features in an app to help with career choice are: booking calls with experts, searching for career experts, and viewing questions and answers in a forum.
  • Users also seem rather hesitant to use an app like Expert: 25% wouldn't use it at all, 31.3% were unsure about it.

User Interviews

Next, I sought out college students for interviews to gain a deeper understanding of their needs and goals. Through guerrilla research and recruitment on a local university campus, I was able to find 5 interviewees and conduct the interviews on location.

Interviews were aimed to find out:

  • How do users go about searching for career advice online and what are their experiences?
  • What pain points do they encounter in tools they use?
  • What do users need to know about experts to trust them and contact them for advice?
  • In what case would they use messaging or video calling to contact experts?

Affinity Mapping

After the transcription of interviews, I used affinity mapping to find common patterns in user's responses. Affinity maps were later used to generate insights.

Key Findings

Can't find relevant information

To make a career choice, users want to find information on entry requirements, work tasks, salary, and promotion opportunities. However, they can't find this information when they look for careers online.

Search as entry barrier

When users don't have a clear idea about their careers or as foreigners, it can be hard to think of search terms and know what to look for. Search is further complicated by mismatched results.

Vague information & complex language

When users want to make sense of the information on the site, they face obstacles like vague information and complex language in job ads. This makes it difficult for them to know what to expect and decide if it's worth to pursue the profession.

Messaging first, video calling second

For initial contact, users would prefer to message the expert, since they don't know him yet. Video calling seems to be rather suited for later when they have established a relationship with the expert.

About me, professional experience & reviews

Users would look at the about me sections to get a first impression on the expert, check his professional experience, and read reviews of others to estimate if the expert is trustworthy before they contact him for advice.

Experts should have industry knowledge

Experts should not be only knowledgeable in career consulting for users to turn to them, but also have industry knowledge in related fields users seek advice in. Such experts would rather be able to answer specific questions on a certain profession.

POV

Empathizing with the User

Using my research findings, I created personas to emphasize with the users and understand their needs, goals, and pain points. As a reference throughout the project, personas helped me to stay on track and make design decisions with end-users in mind.

Ideate

Mapping out the Journey

Next, I moved on to ideation. I used my personas to create user journey maps in order to visualize the process Isabel and Tim need to go through to accomplish their goals in Expert:

Isabel Lange wants to decide on her best job fit and will use Expert to browse experts, read their about me and professional experience and message the expert for advice on what profession might fit her best.

Tim Becker has a specific job role in mind and needs to weigh benefits and costs to get a realistic overview of the role. He will use Expert to search for an IT expert, check reviews on him and book a call with the expert for more inside advice in order to make a decision.

Based on journey maps, key features for Expert were roughly decided:

  • Sign Up
  • Search for an expert
  • Send a message to an expert
  • Book a call with an expert

User Flows

Using the journey maps and personas as a guide, I created user flows to understand the specific tasks Tim and Isabel would need to do in the app and the pages needed to complete these tasks.

User Flow for Finding an Expert

Information Architecture

Based on my user flows and journey maps, I created the first version of a sitemap. To validate the sitemap, I conducted an open card sort with 6 participants via OptimalWorkshop to find out how users would organize information that feels logical to them.

Card Sort Results

Most of the main groupings in the initial sitemap were intuitive to participants. Some sections were labeled differently, such as FAQ and Upgrade. Participants also grouped the two types of experts (career and IT experts) into one category along with a list of experts and search, instead of creating separate categories for each one.

Results lead to a revision of the sitemap with the following changes:
  • Remove expert categories and make two types of experts searchable on one page.
  • Relabel FAQ to Help and Upgrade to Payment to reflect user's mental model.
  • Link video calls to expert profile.

Prototype

Sketching out possible solutions

With my sitemap, user flows, and personas in hand, I started creating low-to-high-fidelity wireframes for the key features by rapidly prototyping different solutions.

At first, I created rough low-fidelity sketches on paper, which were later turned into mid-and high-fidelity wireframes and prototypes using Sketch and Figma. At the end of this phase, I had a high-fidelity prototype that was ready to be tested with real users.

Low-fidelity wireframes

Findings from my research gave me a good idea of what users would expect on an expert profile. To offer Tim and Isabel the simplest way possible to contact experts I included two buttons on the expert's profile to either message experts directly or to book calls with them.

For the experts' page, the initial idea was to lay out expert cards horizontally including expert's name, a related tag, a preview of his about me, and a view profile button, to save space and display more cards on the page. Through feedback, I later realized the cards were too small to account for longer expert names and multiple tags. So I changed the layout of cards in later iterations to stretch them out vertically.

Test

Usability Testing

To validate the solution, I conducted moderated remote usability tests with 6 participants via skype. The goal of these tests was to assess the learnability and errors for first-time users.

  • Do users understand the web app and its value?
  • How easy can they complete Expert's basic functions for the first time?
  • What errors do occur frequently and how severe are they?

Errors were measured using Jakob Nielsen's rating scale. I also measured satisfaction using the single-ease-question at the end of each test by asking participants to rate the difficulty of tasks.

Test Results

All participants found the app simple, straightforward, and easy to use. They could complete 3 out of 4 tasks quickly and easily. The major area of confusion was task 2 (search for expert) and the lack of transparency for costs of video calls. The main challenges and errors experienced are listed below.

View test report View test script
Irrelevant Search Terms (Severity 2)

5 out of 6 users found the search terms irrelevant to their search. They would have directly searched for "software developer" to find the expert, instead of picking an IT expert from the suggestions. Two users were also confused about the difference between IT expert and IT consultant.

Change

I replaced the search terms with job roles and included software developer. I also changed the helper text in the search bar on experts' page to "enter job roles" instead of "enter field of work", which was a bit ambiguous.

Check Plans Button (Severity 3)

Users were frustrated to find "check plans" button didn't work on the book a call modal, which informs them about the paid feature. 3 out of 6 users clicked on the button several times when they first encountered it. Users wanted to be more informed about the costs of calls before they book them.

Change

I created a new payment page and linked it to the check plans button. This page contains an overview of monthly plans and a FAQ section with the most common questions regarding payment. Users can now check the costs for calls and can feel more informed before booking calls.

Visual Design

Bringing Expert to Life

After having fixed flaws in the prototype through usability testing feedback, it was time to bring Expert to life with visuals. To refine the visual design for Expert, I made use of gestalt principles and color psychology.

Color Choice

As Expert is career-related, I wanted to keep the design minimal, professional, and visually appealing at the same time. To evoke emotions of hope and optimism in users who are unsure about their career paths, I chose a color palette with blue and yellow tones. While the yellow-orange color stands for the optimism of users to make a career choice, the blue tones represent professionalism, trust and expertise of experts.

To ensure consistency across our brand, I created a design system. Finally, the design was iterated based on peer feedback and accessibility guidelines.

Learnings

The expert project was a valuable experience in many respects. Going through the whole design process from start to finish brought not only joy but was also challenging and frustrating at times. Through multiple rounds of iterations and trial-and-error on user flows, journey maps, and wireframes, I learned to be open to feedback and embrace changes to ultimately improve the product for users.

I also learned to resist the temptation to add a lot of features, but stay focused on the core ones, that would be valuable for users. As Expert is an MVP, new features will be added and validated in upcoming iterations such as a payment flow for PayPal, search filters, and the option to leave reviews on experts.

Finally, I learned that the quality of your research depends on the quality of your questions. In hindsight, I would focus on user tasks more in interviews and test mid-fidelity prototypes before moving on to high-fidelity ones.
